Study at a glance

Why this paper matters

Open-source ecosystems depend on sustained contributor participation, yet sudden and negative withdrawal remains poorly understood. This study investigates whether socio-technical conditions around contributors can help explain abrupt disengagement events.

The analysis models disengagement as a measurable empirical outcome and tests how communication breakdowns, structural fragmentation, and developer embeddedness relate to short-, medium-, and long-term risk.

Research questions

RQ1

Can abrupt disengagement be predicted using community smells?

Research questions

RQ2

Can abrupt disengagement be predicted by socio-technical proximity metrics?

Operationalization

Outcome

A contributor is considered abruptly disengaged when a negative final message is followed by at least three months of inactivity.

Method

Research design

01

Project selection

Six Apache Software Foundation projects with public code, issues, mailing-list discussions, and long-enough development history.

02

Data extraction

Artifacts collected from GitHub, JIRA, and Apache Pony Mail, then unified at developer level across 30-day windows.

03

Feature construction

Community smells and socio-technical network measures derived using Kaiaulu and OSLOM-based community analysis.

04

Modeling

Logistic regression models estimated over multiple temporal horizons: next month, next three months, and next year.

Main findings

What the paper shows

Isolation matters fast

Lone Wolf and Radio Silence are associated with higher short-term risk of abrupt disengagement.

Structural silos act slowly

Organizational Silo becomes more visible as a long-term risk factor rather than an immediate trigger.

Embeddedness can protect

Higher socio-technical alignment and lower communication distance reduce early exit risk.

Too much interaction can backfire

Dense communication can become overload, suggesting that not all collaboration intensity is beneficial.
